4.4.2 Financing Structure and Funding Sources 4.4.2 融资结构与资金来源

The Budapest–Belgrade Railway exhibits a pronounced "blended finance" characteristic that distinguishes it sharply from the Piraeus case. Total project investment is approximately $2.89 billion, involving five distinct financing parties with mutually incompatible rule systems. 布达佩斯-贝尔格莱德铁路呈现出明显的"混合融资"特征，这与比雷埃夫斯港案例形成鲜明对比。项目总投资约28.9亿美元，涉及五个具有互不相容规则体系的不同融资方。

The "Compliance Impossible Triangle":

This financing structure's rule diversity index significantly exceeds that of Piraeus (primarily single-source). 4.4.3 Rule Interaction Morphology: Formation Mechanisms of Conflictual Confrontation 4.4.3 规则互动形态：冲突性对抗的形成机制

The formation of conflictual confrontation in the Budapest–Belgrade Railway stems from the cumulative failure of three layers: financing coordination, standard alignment, and review management. 布达佩斯-贝尔格莱德铁路冲突性对抗的形成源于三个层面的累积失败：融资协调、标准对接和审查管理。

Table 4-9: Three Layers of Mechanism Failure

1 Layer 1: Financing Coordination Failure

Manifestation: Multiple financiers unable to agree on rule prioritization; China insists on loan conditions (linked to Chinese contractors), EU insists on procurement rules (international open tendering).

Missing Critical Condition: Absence of rule arbitration mechanism; no party capable of forced coordination.

Contrast with Piraeus: Piraeus financing structure relatively simple, requiring no multi-party coordination.

2 Layer 2: Standard Alignment Failure

Manifestation: Chinese railway technical standards vs. EU TSI (Technical Specifications for Interoperability) standards; differences in signaling systems, safety certification, technical parameters.

Missing Critical Condition: Absence of technical mutual recognition agreements; lack of China-EU railway standards dialogue mechanism.

Contrast with Piraeus: Piraeus technical standards (port operations) have smaller differences with EU standards, compensable through local procurement.

3 Layer 3: Review Management Failure

Manifestation: EU Commission investigation procedure uncertainty amplified; 2017 investigation remains unconcluded to date (2026), creating expectation confusion.

Missing Critical Condition: Absence of review time limits; host country (Hungary) wavers between EU pressure and Chinese commitments.

Contrast with Piraeus: Piraeus EU review (competition review) had clear time limits and conclusions, high predictability.

4.4.4 Rule Conflicts and EU Review 4.4.4 规则冲突与欧盟审查

Since its launch, the Budapest–Belgrade Railway has encountered multiple EU-level rule reviews, serving as a paradigm case of BRI facing "institutional barriers" in Europe. 自启动以来，布达佩斯-贝尔格莱德铁路遭遇了多次欧盟层面的规则审查，成为BRI在欧洲面临"制度壁垒"的范例案例。

January 2017

EU Commission Investigation Launch

European Commission launched investigation focusing on: (1) Project financial viability; (2) Potential violations of EU law requiring "large transport projects to be publicly tendered."

Key issue: Hungary (EU member) bound by EU law; Serbia (candidate) applies relaxed rules. Differential applicability created legal risks—fines and prosecution possible.

2018–2019

Technical Standard Negotiation Deadlock

Project originally planned to adopt Chinese railway technical standards; EU requires TSI compliance for network entry.

Serbian PM Vucic stated: "Serbia has no obstacles provided they comply with EU standards." Industry note: Final products show no difference, but process parameters and product specifications diverge.

2020–2021

Financing Coordination Stalemate

EU fund application submitted, creating mutual exclusivity with Chinese loan conditions. Open tendering requirement vs. Chinese contractor linkage.

2022–Present

Geopolitical Complication

Russia-Ukraine war sensitized Russian loan component. "De-risking" framework intensified scrutiny of Chinese infrastructure investment.

Hungarian section remains in "design tendering completed, main works not commenced" status as of 2026.

Nonlinear Cost Multiplication Effect

When projects involve more than three rule-setting parties, and rules address core technical standards (rather than peripheral procedural matters), compliance costs increase exponentially rather than linearly. The Budapest–Belgrade Railway crossed this threshold.

Core Theoretical Finding: Variability of Rule Interaction Morphology

The same BRI project can exhibit different interaction morphologies across different segments (Serbian section vs. Hungarian section of the Budapest–Belgrade Railway), demonstrating that rule interaction is not an inherent project attribute but a context-dependent outcome.

Structural Constraints on Institutional Arbitrage: The "institutional arbitrage" strategies of Kazakhstan and other countries are feasible in medium-density institutional environments, yet contract sharply in high-density environments (such as Hungary as an EU member); institutional density is the boundary condition for arbitrage possibility.

4.4.8 Validation of Institutional Comparison 4.4.8 制度比较验证

The China–Europe Land–Sea Express Line case provides rich evidence for the Chapter 3 institutional comparison, validating multiple dimensions of the theoretical framework: 中欧陆海快线案例为第三章的制度比较提供了丰富证据，验证了理论框架的多个维度：

First: Practical manifestation of rule conflicts.

The project fully demonstrates the rule differences identified in Chapter 3—the BRI's "efficiency first" (desiring rapid advancement) versus the EU's "rules first" (emphasizing open tendering, compliance review) generate direct collisions in practice.

Second: Concretization of "compliance risks."

The "compliance risks" analyzed in Chapter 3.3 are validated—when projects simultaneously involve Chinese financing and EU rules, compliance costs increase significantly and project cycles extend.

Third: Limited space for "institutional arbitrage."

Serbia as a non-EU country could apply more relaxed rules, yet as an EU candidate country, it must still converge toward EU standards. This indicates that in EU neighborhood regions, "institutional arbitrage" space is limited.

Fourth: Practical challenges of "rules-based governance."

The EU emphasizes transparency, open tendering, and compliance review, yet this also leads to project implementation lag. "High standards mean high time consumption" poses challenges to Global Gateway strategic effectiveness.

Fifth: Urgency of China–Europe rule coordination.

The Budapest–Belgrade predicament demonstrates the urgency of rule coordination. If both sides could establish cooperation mechanisms for standard mutual recognition, procurement rule alignment, and financing coordination, execution efficiency would substantially improve.
